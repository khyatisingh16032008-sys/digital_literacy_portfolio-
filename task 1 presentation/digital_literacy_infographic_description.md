# Task 1 – Digital Literacy Awareness Infographic

## Tool Used
**Canva** (canva.com) — free browser-based design platform

## What the Infographic Shows

The infographic is titled **"Your Digital Literacy Starter Kit"** and is designed as a single A4-size visual card for first-year B.Tech students. It covers three core topics:

1. **What is Digital Literacy?**
   A short definition box explaining that digital literacy is the ability to find, evaluate, create, and communicate information using digital technologies safely and responsibly.

2. **Safe Internet Practices**
   An icon grid covering: using strong passwords, avoiding public Wi-Fi for banking, recognising phishing links, enabling two-factor authentication, and keeping software updated.

3. **Professional Online Presence**
   A checklist showing students how to present themselves online — using a formal profile photo, keeping bios professional, avoiding controversial posts, and linking to academic work.

The design uses a blue-and-white colour scheme with simple flat icons, making it easy to read at a glance.

## Reflection (150–200 words)

Creating this infographic was more challenging than I expected. I initially wanted to include all five topics from the brief, but quickly realised that cramming too much content made the design look cluttered and hard to read. The most interesting part was learning how Canva's grid and frame layouts work — I had never used a professional design tool before this assignment. I decided to focus on three topics and give each enough visual space so that the content could be understood without reading lengthy text.

One difficulty was choosing icons that accurately represented abstract ideas like "digital literacy." I ended up using a combination of a laptop, a lightbulb, and a shield icon to represent learning, ideas, and online safety respectively. Another challenge was selecting a colour scheme — I wanted the infographic to look clean and academic rather than colourful and childish, so I settled on a navy blue and white palette with teal highlights. Overall, the task taught me that effective visual communication requires making deliberate choices about what to leave out, not just what to include.
